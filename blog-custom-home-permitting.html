Ground-Up Custom Home Construction: The Permitting Process Explained

Joe Shadel · December 2025

Building a custom home is one of the most rewarding projects a homeowner can undertake — and one of the most complex to navigate from a regulatory standpoint. After 40 years in the business, I can tell you this: understanding the permitting process before you break ground saves money, time, and significant frustration.

Every municipality is different.

Zoning ordinances, building department staffing, and inspection schedules vary by jurisdiction. What's straightforward in one town may require a variance in the next. Your contractor needs to know the specific rules in your specific municipality — not just state building code in general.

The permit sequence for a new custom home

A typical ground-up build requires multiple permits and inspections issued in sequence:

  1. Zoning approval — confirming the proposed structure complies with setbacks, lot coverage, and height limits
  2. Building permit — issued after plans are reviewed and approved
  3. Foundation inspection — before concrete is poured
  4. Framing inspection — before insulation or drywall
  5. Rough-in inspections — plumbing, electrical, and HVAC each inspected separately
  6. Insulation inspection — before drywall
  7. Final inspections — plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and building
  8. Certificate of Occupancy — issued when all inspections pass

Depending on plan review backlog and inspection scheduling, the full permit-to-CO process on a custom home can take 12 to 18 months from first submission to move-in. Planning for this timeline from the start is essential.

Variances and planning board approvals

If your project exceeds any zoning ordinance — a common situation on large luxury estates — you'll need a variance from the local zoning board. This typically adds two to four months for the application, hearing, and approval process. An experienced contractor who has appeared before these boards can help you prepare a strong application.

Environmental approvals

Many properties — particularly larger rural lots — have wetlands, steep slopes, or environmental restrictions that require separate regulatory review before a building permit can be issued. Identifying these constraints early prevents costly surprises.
